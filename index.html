Electroencephalographic Assessment of Comfort in Urban Environments Using Virtual Reality

EEGFlow Research Group

Independent Study

Abstract

The impact of urban environments on human well-being has become an urgent issue in this rapidly urbanizing society. Epidemiological studies consistently suggest an increased risk of mental disorders among urban residents. In response, neurourbanism has been proposed as a fusion of neuroscience and urban planning. However, existing research often remains limited to passive viewing experiments using static images or videos, failing to fully elucidate the dynamic effects of various urban environmental factors on comfort. This study employed a novel experimental system combining immersive virtual reality (VR) technology with high-coverage electroencephalography (64-channel EEG) to quantify comfort in Japan's dense urban environments. Results suggest that green visibility ratio is associated with "physical calmness," primarily manifested as reduced heart rate, while the overall building height may be linked to "attention/arousal (cognitive engagement)," indicated by an increase in beta band power. Meanwhile, building coverage showed the most consistent influence on the subjective recovery rating (PRS). Therefore, these results suggest that the comfort of urban landscapes can manifest different aspects through distinct indicators such as "relaxation (physical response)," "engagement (neural activity)," and "recovery (subjective feeling)." Consequently, the design of green visibility ratio and high-rise development may need to be adjusted according to the objective - whether prioritizing rest or emphasizing vitality and stimulation.

Index TermsNeurourbanism; virtual reality; EEG; urban environment; comfort.

1Introduction

Urban environments significantly influence mental health, but physiological research is limited. The "neurourbanism" framework [1,3,5] evaluates environmental effects using EEG and autonomic indicators. Previous studies used static images or videos [4,6], limiting realistic experiences. This study combines immersive VR with 64-channel EEG to systematically control four urban factors (Coverage, Height, Green Visibility, Noise) and quantify their effects on comfort using physiological and subjective measures.

2Methods

Sixteen participants (9 females, 7 males; age: 20.6 +/- 1.4 years) experienced VR urban environments using Meta Quest 3 while EEG (64-ch actiCHamp, Brain Products) and ECG were recorded. Stimuli were created in Unreal Engine 5, with four parameters varying at high and low levels (2^4 = 16 conditions). Each trial: Baseline (30 s) -> VR stimulus (3 min) -> Rest (30 s). Subjective restoration was assessed using the Perceived Restorativeness Scale (PRS). EEG power (Delta, Theta, Alpha, Beta, Gamma) was calculated from O1, O2, F3, F4 electrodes. Heart rate (HR) and HRV (RMSSD) were computed from ECG. Statistical analyses used paired t-tests and linear mixed models with FDR correction.

3Results

Heart rate significantly decreased during Rest (t = -2.086, p = 0.037). For environmental factors: (1) Height showed a trend toward increased Beta power (p = 0.056, d = 0.24), suggesting cognitive arousal; (2) Green Visibility significantly reduced HR (p = 0.037; Delta HR: -2.83 bpm, p = 0.019), indicating relaxation; (3) Coverage showed the strongest effect on subjective PRS (F = 4.57, p = 0.051), with significant effects on Fascination, Scope, Compatibility, and Preference subscales. Coverage and Noise showed no significant physiological effects.

4Discussion

Results suggest "Functional Divergence" of urban elements: Green Visibility promotes physical relaxation (HR reduction), supporting Stress Reduction Theory; Height induces cognitive arousal (Beta increase) without stress, reflecting "Fascination" in Attention Restoration Theory [2]; Coverage most consistently affects subjective restoration. These findings enable evidence-based urban design - prioritizing greenery for rest areas and building height for stimulating environments. Limitations include marginal significance levels requiring larger samples and short-term VR exposure.

5Conclusion

This neurourbanism study demonstrates that urban environmental factors differentially affect physiological and subjective responses: Green Visibility promotes autonomic relaxation, Height induces cognitive engagement, and Coverage influences perceived restoration. These findings support designing urban spaces with targeted effects on body and brain.
